What is COVID-19?

The 2019 Novel Coronavirus (COVID-19) is a newly discovered and infectious disease caused by a virus spread from person to person. COVID-19 is part of a family of coronaviruses, which are a large family of viruses that cause illness to animals and humans.

Where did COVID-19 originate?

COVID-19 was first discovered in Wuhan City, China when an outbreak of the virus began in December 2019. The original infections were linked to a live animal market and the virus is suspected by scientists to have originated from bats or pangolins and transmitted to humans.

How does it spread?

COVID-19 is primarily spread through:

  • Person to person contact within 6 feet’s distance

  • Respiratory (water) droplets that are produced when an infected person coughs, sneezes or talks

  • Touching an infected surface or object and then touching your mouth, nose or eyes

What are the symptoms?

There are a wide-range of symptoms that people experience after being exposed to COVID-19. These symptoms may appear anywhere between 2-14 days after being exposed to the virus. Severity levels of these symptoms vary from person to person and are dependent on many factors (mainly age and underlying health conditions).


Common Symptoms

  • Cough

  • Fever or chills

  • Shortness of breath or difficulty breathing

  • Fatigue

  • Muscle or body aches

  • Nasal congestion or runny nose

  • Headache

  • Loss of taste or smell

  • Sore throat

  • Nausea or vomiting

  • Diarrhea

  • Skin rash or discoloration of toes / fingers

*** Some people may be presymptomatic (having COVID-19 before developing any symptoms) or asymptomatic (having COVID-19 but develop no symptoms)

Is there a cure or vaccine?

There is currently no known cure or vaccine for COVID-19. Moderna, AstraZeneca, Pfizer, Johnson & Johnson and many other companies around the world are coordinating clinical trials and conducting ongoing efforts to develop vaccines and medications to help prevent and treat COVID-19.


You can find updated information on vaccines here

Is COVID-19 dangerous? If so, why?

There are several reasons why COVID-19 is a dangerous disease. Although COVID-19 is not the most deadly disease in history, there are several reasons why it should be taken serious:


Why should I take COVID-19 serious?

  • It is highly contagious and can be spread easily from person to person

  • There is currently no proven cure or vaccine for the virus

  • No one is immune to the virus

  • It can be fatal for people who are of old age or have underlying health conditions

  • The virus targets the respiratory tract (lungs & bronchial tubes) which is responsible for your breathing

How do I protect myself and others?

Although there is no cure or vaccine proven to treat COVID-19, there are several preventive measures you can take to help reduce the chances of being infected or spreading the virus. Below are some of the most common preventive measures you can take.


Preventive Measures

  • Wearing a mask or face cloth covering over your mouth and nose in public settings and areas

    • *** Masks or cloth face coverings should not be placed on young children under age 2, anyone who has trouble breathing, or anyone not able to remove the covering without help ***

  • Maintaining a social distance of 6-feet from others in public settings and areas

  • Avoiding crowded places (bars, parties, restaurants, etc.) where the virus can easily spread

  • Limiting in-person contact as much as possible

  • Completing daily, in-person activities online or through a virtual setting

  • Avoiding contact or interactions with people you know who test positive for COVID-19 or develop symptoms

  • Avoiding contact or interactions with people who are not wearing masks or cloth face coverings

  • Cleaning and disinfecting personal and household items and frequently touched surfaces (a complete guide on disinfection can be found here)

  • Washing your hands often using soap & water for at least 20 seconds OR using alcohol-based sanitizer that contains at least 60% alcohol (some handwashing tips can be found here)

  • Covering your mouth with either a tissue or the inside of you elbow whenever you cough or sneeze

  • Monitoring your health daily by being alert for any COVID-19 symptoms you may develop and taking your temperature often

How does testing work for COVID-19?

There are two kinds of tests available for COVID-19: viral tests and antibody tests. A viral test tells you if you have a current infection and an antibody test tells you if you had a previous infection. You can receive testing for one or the other, or both.


Viral & Antibody Tests

Antibody tests are done by taking the patient’s blood using a finger prick or needle. Viral tests are done by taking swabs of either the patient’s mouth, throat, middle nostrils, or front nostrils.

For viral tests, you can either test:

  • Positive (you “most likely” do currently have COVID-19)

  • Negative (you “most likely” do NOT currently have COVID-19)

For antibody tests, you can either test:

  • Positive (you “most likely” have had COVID-19 in the past)

  • Negative (you “most likely” NEVER had COVID-19 in the past)

And for BOTH tests, you can either test:

  • Viral Positive & Antibody Positive (you “most likely” do currently have COVID-19)

  • Viral Positive & Antibody Negative (you “most likely” do currently have COVID-19)

  • Viral Negative & Antibody Positive (you “likely” have had & recovered COVID-19)

  • Viral Negative & Antibody Negative (you “likely” NEVER had COVID-19)

*** If you test positive or negative for COVID-19 on a viral or an antibody test, you still should take preventive measures to protect yourself and others. Guidance on interpreting your testing results can be found here.

Where can I get tested?

If you have symptoms of COVID-19 and want to get tested, call your doctor or healthcare provider first. Your doctor or healthcare provider can give you information on where and how to get tested or you can visit your state or local health department’s website. Most testing is done in local healthcare centers (either in-person or drive-through).

At-Home Testing

As of July 15, 2020, The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) has also authorized “at-home test kits” available for purchase from eight different companies:

  • Everlywell

These at-home tests allow you to collect either a nasal swab or saliva sample at home and send them back or to a local laboratory for analysis.

***It’s important to note that testing is not 100% accurate, which is why it is important for you to let your doctor or healthcare provider review your test results to confirm your current condition and help determine any next steps

What do I do if I appear to have symptoms or test positive for COVID-19?

*** If your symptoms appear to be severe or you develop these emergency warning signs from COVID-19:

  • Trouble breathing

  • Persistent pain or pressure in the chest

  • New confusion

  • Inability to wake or stay awake

  • Blush lips or face


Seek emergency medical care IMMEDIATELY. Make sure to call 911 or your local emergency facility and notify the operator that you are seeking care for COVID-19.


If you appear to have developed mild COVID-19 symptoms or test positive for the disease, below are several things you should do.



Testing positive for COVID-19

  • Stay at home if you appear to have symptoms and only leave for medical care

  • Call your doctor or healthcare provider to tell them about your symptoms (or if you’ve tested positive for COVID-19) and schedule an appointment

  • When visiting your doctor or healthcare provider, make sure to follow preventative measures for COVID-19 and avoid public transportation (uber, taxis, subway, lyft, ride-share, carpool, etc.)

  • Monitor your symptoms over time and follow care instructions from your doctor or healthcare provider

  • Take care of yourself by getting enough rest, staying hydrated, taking over-the-counter medication (acetaminophen for fever or pain) and more

  • Seperate yourself from other people and pets in your household and absolutely limit your interactions with them (more information on this can be found here and here)

  • If possible, stay in a specific room and use a separate bathroom if you live in a household with other people

  • Wear a mask or cloth covering and maintain a social distance of at least 6 feet as much as possible when around family members in your household

  • In your isolated “sick” room, make sure to clean and disinfect commonly touched surfaces such as phones, counters, tabletops, doorknobs, keyboards, tablets, and bedside tables (a full list of disinfectants you can use can be found here)

  • Avoid sharing common or personal household items with other people in your household (ex: dishes, bedding, clothes, etc.) and make sure to wash and clean these items thoroughly after use

  • Cover your mouth and nose with a tissue when you cough or sneeze and immediately wash your hands in a separate bathroom afterwards

  • Clean your hands often (handwashing tips can be found here)

More information and advice to help you care for yourself can be found here. Use CDC's self-checker tool to help you determine if you need to seek medical care.

How do I care for my loved ones or family members who are sick?

*** If their symptoms appear to be severe, or they develop these emergency warning signs from COVID-19:

  • Trouble breathing

  • Persistent pain or pressure in the chest

  • New confusion

  • Inability to wake or stay awake

  • Blush lips or face


Seek emergency medical care IMMEDIATELY. Make sure to call 911 or your local emergency facility and notify the operator that the person you are caring for needs care for COVID-19.


If someone you are caring for appears to have developed mild COVID-19 symptoms or tests positive for the disease, there are several things you should do:


Caring for someone with COVID-19

  • If you are living in a household with multiple people, the caregiver should NOT be someone who is at a higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19

  • Monitor their symptoms and seek medical care if severe symptoms or illness develops

  • Have the person you are caring for remain at home and only transport them out the household if they are need of medical care

  • Make sure to contact the doctor or healthcare provider of that person to describe their symptoms (or if they’ve tested positive for COVID-19) and schedule an appointment if possible

  • Seperate yourself from the person you’re caring for and limit close contact between them as much as possible (maintain a social distance of at least 6 feet)

  • Make sure that the person you are caring for has a separate bedroom (“sick room”) and bathroom in the household if possible

  • Avoid sharing common or personal household items with the person you’re caring for (ex: dishes, bedding, clothes, etc.)

  • If possible, provide the person you are caring for with trash bags, masks or cloth face coverings and cleaning supplies to use for their bedroom and bathroom

  • Wear a mask or cloth face covering and disposable gloves when caring for the person in any way, dealing with the person’s laundry, disposing their trash, cleaning their bathroom or bedroom, and handling their dishes and silverware (try providing them with plastic plates and utensils to dispose of instead)

  • When entering the same room as the person you are caring for, ensure that they are wearing a mask or cloth face covering

  • Clean your hands more often (handwashing tips can be found here)

  • Track your own health while caring for the person (use CDC's self-checker tool tool to help you make decisions about seeking appropriate medical care for yourself)

More advice for caregivers can be found here.

How do I know if I should quarantine or isolate?

There is a slight difference when it comes to determining if you need to quarantine or isolate because of COVID-19. It is recommended that you isolate yourself if you have COVID-19 and quarantine if you MIGHT have COVID-19.

Quarantine

You should quarantine yourself if:

  • You’ve been in close contact with someone who has COVID-19

  • You’ve previously had COVID-19 (taken the antibody test and tested positive)

*** It is recommended that you stay home for 14 days after your last contact with a person who has COVID-19 before going out

Isolation

You should isolate yourself if:

  • You test positive for COVID-19

  • You have symptoms of COVID-19 and are able to recover at home

  • You are asymptomatic (show no symptoms of COVID-19) but have tested positive

If you fall within either one of these categories, it is important that you are taking care of yourself and others (if needed) at home as much as possible

*** Consult with your doctor or healthcare provider if you’re unsure of which option is best for you.

When is it safe for me to leave my house or be around others after testing positive for COVID-19?

If you tested positive for COVID-19, it is recommended that before leaving your house or being around others you:

  • Wait at least 10 days since your symptoms first appeared

  • Be able to go at least 24 hours without any fever or the use of fever-reducing medication

  • Have improved symptoms


What if I tested positive for COVID-19 but showed no symptoms?

If you tested positive for COVID-19 but had no symptoms, it is recommended that before leaving your house or being around others you wait at least 10 days after you’ve tested for COVID-19.

*** Consult with your doctor or healthcare provider if you’re unsure whether or not it’s safe for you to leave your house or be with others

Is COVID-19 fatal?

Yes, COVID-19 can be fatal. There have been approx. 810,000+ deaths worldwide due to COVID-19 and approx. 175,000+ deaths in the United States alone (as of August 25, 2020).

However, the COVID-19 recovery rate is between 97% and 99.75%. Of the approx. 23.7 million confirmed cases worldwide, approx. 15.4 million people have recovered from COVID-19 (as of August 25, 2020).

The fatality rate from COVID-19 is the highest amongst adults 65 years old and older and people with underlying health conditions. According to the CDC, “8 out of 10 COVID-19 deaths reported in the U.S. have been in adults 65 years old and older”.

How do I know if I’m at high risk for severe illness from COVID-19?

Older adults (65 and older) and people with underlying medical conditions are at a higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19. Studies have shown that as you get older, your risk for severe illness from COVID-19 increases. Adults aged 85 or older have the greatest risk for severe illness from COVID-19 compared to children, teens and young adults who pose the least risk for severe illness from COVID-19. Below are lists of underlying medical conditions that can make you at a higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19.


High Risk

People with the following underlying medical conditions ARE at higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19:

  • Cancer

  • Chronic kidney disease

  • COPD (chronic obstructive pulmonary disease)

  • Immunocompromised state (weakened immune system) from solid organ transplant

  • Obesity (body mass index [BMI] of 30 or higher)

  • Serious heart conditions, such as heart failure, coronary artery disease, or cardiomyopathies

  • Sickle cell disease

  • Type 2 diabetes mellitus

***It is important to note that if you fall within any of these high-risk groups, you should take extra preventive measures to avoid getting COVID-19. This list of underlying conditions may be updated as new information about COVID-19 evolves over time. Stay updated on this list here.

Possible High Risk

People with the following underlying medical conditions MIGHT be at higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19:

  • Asthma (moderate-to-severe)

  • Cerebrovascular disease (affects blood vessels and blood supply to the brain)

  • Cystic fibrosis

  • Hypertension or high blood pressure

  • Immunocompromised state (weakened immune system) from blood or bone marrow transplant, immune deficiencies, HIV, use of corticosteroids, or use of other immune weakening medicines

  • Neurologic conditions, such as dementia

  • Liver disease

  • Pregnancy

  • Pulmonary fibrosis (having damaged or scarred lung tissues)

  • Smoking

  • Thalassemia (a type of blood disorder)

  • Type 1 diabetes mellitus

***It is important to note that if you fall within any of these high-risk groups, you should take extra preventive measures to avoid getting COVID-19. This list of underlying conditions may be updated as new information about COVID-19 evolves over time. Stay updated on this list here.

Possible High Risk (Children)

Children with the following underlying medical conditions MIGHT be at higher risk for severe illness from COVID-19:

  • Neurologic conditions

  • Genetic conditions

  • Metabolic conditions

  • Congenital heart disease

***It is important to note that if you fall within any of these high-risk groups, you should take extra preventive measures to avoid getting COVID-19. This list of underlying conditions may be updated as new information about COVID-19 evolves over time. Stay updated on this list here.

Who is most at risk for COVID-19?

People of all ages can be infected by COVID-19. Those who don’t wear masks or cloth face coverings in public settings, break social distancing guidelines, participate in large crowds or gatherings, come in contact with others who test positive for COVID-19 or have COVID-19 symptoms and don’t practice good personal hygiene (ex: washing hands) are most at risk for COVID-19.

How long does it take to recover?

For people with mild symptoms and illness from COVID-19, recovery can take approx. 2 weeks. For people with more severe symptoms and illness from COVID-19, recovery can take 3-6 weeks.

*** Consult with your doctor or healthcare provider if you’re unsure of whether or not you’ve recovered from COVID-19

What are the long term effects of COVID-19?

There is much less information known about the long term effects of COVID-19 since the virus was discovered in December 2019. Because COVID-19 acts like a respiratory disease (affecting the lungs), people with severe symptoms or illness from COVID-19 could develop permanent lung damage or fibrosis. Lung damage could also affect the function of other organs such as the heart, kidneys and brain.

What does it mean to “flatten the curve”?

The COVID-19 "curve" is a term used to describe two curves that are graphed on a statistical chart that describes what the spread of the virus looks like with and without protective measures. The dotted line on the graph represents the peak capacity of the health care system to care for a projected number of patients.


Click here for more information

Having steep curves means that there is a surge in COVID-19 cases and a lot of people will need medical care at the same time. Having smooth curves means that there is a slow spread of COVID-19 and much less cases, meaning that people won’t be needing significant medical care at the same time.

To “flatten the curve” means to slow the spread of COVID-19. If everyone were to take preventive measures such as wearing masks & cloth face covering, maintaining social distancing and more, we can be able to slow the spread of COVID-19 and help save millions of lives.
